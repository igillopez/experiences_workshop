The frame

Experiences may not be a financial game changer, but they could become a powerful lever for retention, customer memories and differentiation beyond the booking.

The plan

The path forward is incremental, and it starts by understanding what kind of experience we're selling and where in the journey we're selling it.

1

The "what" — how we could slice the catalog

A useful way to read the catalog emerged: not all "experiences" are the same — some are add-ons, some are real experiences, and that distinction changes everything about how and where we sell them.

Add-ons

City passes, transport cards.

Easy to sell: few inputs, no complex availability — low friction. They're not the "why" of the trip though, so they don't build memories or retention on their own.

Real experiences

Disneyland, Sagrada Familia, Vatican, concerts, F1.

The reason for the trip, or the thing that makes it memorable. The trade-off: they often need date, time, language and passenger data, which adds friction.

A second cut: integration complexity

Pre-package with experiences

Audience-driven funnel

A new parallel audience-driven funnel, separate from (and not modifying) the current DP flow. It would target inspirational audiences on social media (Disneyland, F1, concerts) with dedicated landing pages directing them to pre-bundled packages.

Would need opaque rates to build a strong value proposition vs buying components separately — current net rates don't allow it.

Success criteria: acquisition and competitive differentiation, not revenue.

Post-sales

Most natural moment

Conceptually the most natural moment: experiences have short purchase lead time, and many customers buy them close to departure or in destination. Primarily an engagement & retention play, not a revenue one.

We have a competitive advantage vs vertical players (e.g. Get Your Guide): we have customer data, so we can do contextual recommendations, and we could offer a multi-experience cart (current avg 1.3, could push to 2–3) where competitors only allow one product.

Required enablers

Foundations (non-negotiable)

  • Supply integration → Cart API. Central dependency for any sales channel.
  • Our own checkout — today everything goes through their widget.
  • Generic catalog categorization — stays generic if we go multi-supplier.

Improvements on top

  • CDP for personalization, better targeting and offer tailoring.
  • Mobile app dynamism — quicker access to active experience bookings within the trip area.
  • GeoDB & maps — contextually upsell based on customer location.

In-funnel / Checkout

Fake door agreed

Fastest moment to ship once integration is in place. Consensus: restrict to simple add-ons (city passes, transport cards — one click and done) to avoid adding too much friction while the user is already deciding hotel and flight.

Exception: tough-availability products (e.g. Vatican) could leverage FOMO and earn their place despite the extra friction.

Next step: low-effort fake door experiments to test the trade-off between revenue and added funnel friction.

B2B2C / Welfare

Parallel track

Different and critical case: needs full catalog and standalone product capability — both missing today. Biggest opportunity, but requires solving all product topics. Can run in parallel with B2C since they only overlap on supply and financial flow.

Standalone B2C

Deferred · 2–3 years

Decision: we will not pursue a standalone B2C vertical for the next 2–3 years. Focus stays on upselling and strategic pre-packaging.
